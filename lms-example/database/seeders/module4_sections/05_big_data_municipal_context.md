[STEP] The 3 V's — and what they mean for municipalities

## Big Data in the Municipal Context

“Big data” is often described by the **3 V’s**: **Volume**, **Variety**, **Velocity**. For municipalities, “big” is relative: it is less about absolute size and more about whether your **systems, skills, and processes** can handle the data you need for performance management and AI.

| V | Meaning | Municipal examples |
|---|---------|--------------------|
| **Volume** | Large amount of data | Millions of billing transactions; thousands of assets; years of complaint records; hundreds of council documents. |
| **Variety** | Different types: structured, semi-structured, unstructured | Structured: billing DB, spreadsheets. Semi-structured: JSON from an API, XML. Unstructured: PDF reports, emails, social media. |
| **Velocity** | Speed of generation and need for use | Real-time meter reads; daily payment files; monthly SDBIP updates; annual IDP. |

[STEP] What “big” means in practice

## What “Big” Means in Practice for Municipalities

- **You may not need “big data” technology yet.** Many performance and AI use cases can start with **Excel, Google Sheets, or a simple database** if the data is well structured and of reasonable size (e.g. thousands to low hundreds of thousands of rows). The bottleneck is often **quality** and **integration**, not raw volume.
- **Unstructured data is growing.** IDPs, SDBIPs, council reports, and citizen feedback are often in **PDF or Word**. AI (e.g. ChatGPT, Claude) can help extract and structure this—Module 2 and 3 covered this. The “big” part is **managing and governing** these documents so AI has a clean, authorised set to work from.
- **Real-time is not always necessary.** For many KPIs, **monthly or quarterly** is enough. For early warning (e.g. revenue, critical faults), **daily or weekly** may be. Match **velocity** to the decision you need to make.

## Skills and Readiness

SALGA and the course materials note a **lack of big data analytical skills** in many municipalities. You do not need to become a data engineer. You do need to:

- **Understand** where your data lives, who owns it, and whether it is good enough for your KPIs.
- **Collaborate** with IT and data owners to improve quality and access.
- **Use** tools—including AI—to assess, catalog, clean, and validate data within your role.

The **Practical** and **AI Application** sections give you concrete steps. Module 5 and later will add dashboards and more advanced analytics on top of this foundation.
