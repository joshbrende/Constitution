## AI Application: Revenue Collection Prediction

**Tool:** [ChatGPT](https://chat.openai.com) or [Claude](https://claude.ai)

**Important:** Use only **aggregated, non-confidential** data in prompts (e.g. "collection rate 72%", "collection period 210 days"). **Never** put debtor names, account numbers, or ID numbers into public AI. POPI and MFMA require care.

AI cannot **replace** your billing system or run live predictions on your data. It can: (1) **suggest** what to measure and what rules might predict non-payment; (2) **draft** a structure for a "probability of payment" or "collection risk" model that you or IT can implement with **your** data in **your** systems; (3) **generate** a short narrative or briefing note from **aggregated** numbers you provide.

[STEP] Task A — Suggest leading indicators for collection risk

### Task A: Suggest Leading Indicators for Collection Risk

1. Open **ChatGPT** or **Claude** in a new chat.
2. Paste this prompt (you can add your municipality type: metro, district, local):

   > We are a South African municipality. Our current collection rate is around [X]% and our collection period is [Y] days. We want to improve by predicting which accounts are at higher risk of non-payment so we can prioritise follow-up. We only have access to aggregated data and typical billing system fields (no names or IDs in this exercise). List 8–10 **leading indicators** or **signals** we could use to predict collection risk—e.g. ageing bucket, payment history pattern, account type, tariff band. For each, say: (1) the indicator, (2) why it might predict risk, and (3) where we would typically get the data (which system or report). Use a table.

3. **Use the output:** Review with your **Revenue Manager** or **IT**. These indicators can inform: (a) the **rules** for your revenue/debt tracker; (b) a **brief** for a future in-house or vendor **predictive model** that runs on **your** data, **on-premises or in a secure environment**—not in public AI.

[STEP] Task B — Draft a one-page briefing on collection performance

### Task B: Draft a One-Page Briefing on Collection Performance

1. In a **new chat**, provide **aggregated** numbers only. Example:

   > Our municipality’s revenue collection performance this quarter: Collection rate 72% (target 75%); Collection period 210 days (target 90); Debt 90+ days is 40% of total debt; Cost to collect is 5.2% of revenue. Our main challenges are: residential arrears and a backlog in payment arrangements.

2. Then send:

   > Using the numbers above, write a one-page briefing for the Municipal Manager and Mayoral Committee. Include: (1) a short executive summary (2–3 sentences); (2) a bullet list of the main figures and whether they are on or off target; (3) three recommended priority actions for the next quarter; (4) one sentence on what we need from council or other departments. Use formal, professional language suitable for local government.

3. **Use the output:** **Edit** with your **real** context and **approved** messaging. The CFO or MM should **sign off** before it is used. **Do not** paste back any **confidential** or **person-specific** data.

[STEP] Task C — Suggest a simple "when to escalate" rule set

### Task C: Suggest a Simple "When to Escalate" Rule Set

1. In a **new chat**, send:

   > We are a South African municipality with limited resources for debt collection. We have ageing buckets (current, 30, 60, 90+ days) and we can distinguish roughly between high-value and low-value accounts. We want simple **rules** for when to escalate: e.g. when to move from reminder to payment arrangement, or from arrangement to legal. Give 5–7 rules in the form: "IF [condition] THEN [action]." Consider: ageing, value, whether already in arrangement, and fairness (e.g. we do not want to disconnect indigent accounts that are in arrangement). Use a table: Rule number | Condition | Action | Note.

2. **Use the output:** These are **drafts**. Your **credit control** and **revenue** policies (and possibly legal) must **approve** any escalation rules. AI helps you **structure** the thinking; the **municipality** owns the final policy.
