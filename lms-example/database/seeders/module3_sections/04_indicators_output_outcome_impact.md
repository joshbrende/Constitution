[STEP] Output, outcome, and impact — definitions and examples

## Output, Outcome, and Impact Indicators

Indicators are **measureable** ways to track whether we are achieving outputs, outcomes, and impact. Getting the **type** right matters for reporting, audits, and SALGA alignment.

| Type | Definition | Example indicators (municipal) |
|------|------------|-------------------------------|
| **Output** | What we **deliver** or **produce**—tangible, countable. | Number of household water connections; km of road tarred; training sessions held; by-law cases opened and closed. |
| **Outcome** | The **change** or **result** that follows from our outputs—often short to medium term. | Percentage of households with reliable water; reduction in pipe bursts; improvement in collection rate; citizen satisfaction score. |
| **Impact** | **Long-term** change for the community or municipality—often over years. | Improved health and dignity; sustainable revenue base; reduced inequality in access to services; economic growth in the area. |

### Rule of thumb

- **Output** = “We did X.” (We built 500 connections.)
- **Outcome** = “Because we did X, Y changed.” (More households now have reliable water.)
- **Impact** = “In the longer term, the community or municipality is better off.” (Improved health, dignity, and economic activity.)

[STEP] Leading vs lagging indicators

## Leading vs Lagging Indicators

| | **Leading** | **Lagging** |
|---|-------------|-------------|
| **What** | Predict or drive future results; often earlier in the chain. | Measure results after they have occurred. |
| **Example** | % of meters read on time; % of S56 managers with signed performance agreements; number of maintenance backlogs cleared. | Collection rate; audit outcome; citizen satisfaction. |
| **Use** | Early warning; manage during the year. | Year-end reporting; accountability. |

A strong framework uses **both**: leading indicators to steer during the year, lagging indicators to report and account at the end.

[STEP] SMART and good practice

## SMART and Good Practice for Indicators

Indicators should be **SMART** where possible:

- **S**pecific — clear what is being measured.
- **M**easurable — we can get a number or category.
- **A**chievable — realistic given capacity and time.
- **R**elevant — linked to IDP, SDBIP, and SALGA.
- **T**ime-bound — we know the period (e.g. by 30 June; for the financial year).

### Good practice

- **One indicator, one measure.** Avoid double-barrelled indicators (e.g. “Water and sanitation connections” is better split if you report them separately).
- **Define the numerator and denominator** for ratios (e.g. “Collection rate = cash collected / billed amount × 100”).
- **State the data source** (billing system, survey, surveyor report) and **who owns** the indicator.
- **Align to the results chain**: output indicators for outputs; outcome indicators for outcomes. Avoid calling an output an “outcome” — auditors and Treasury notice.
