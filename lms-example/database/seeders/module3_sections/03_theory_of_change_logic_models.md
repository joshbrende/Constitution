[STEP] Theory of Change — the results chain

## Theory of Change (ToC)

A **Theory of Change** is a description of *how* and *why* a set of activities is expected to lead to desired results. It makes the **logic** of your programme explicit: *If we do X, then Y will happen, because Z.*

### The results chain (logic chain)

A typical ToC runs along a **results chain**:

| Level | What it is | Example (water programme) |
|-------|------------|---------------------------|
| **Inputs** | Resources: money, staff, equipment, systems | Budget; engineers; pipes; meters |
| **Activities** | What we do with the inputs | Lay pipes; install meters; maintain networks; run awareness campaigns |
| **Outputs** | Tangible deliverables—what we produce | 500 new household connections; 20 km of pipe laid; 1 000 meters installed |
| **Outcomes** | Short- to medium-term changes—what changes as a result | More households with reliable water; fewer bursts; improved payment compliance |
| **Impact** | Long-term change for the community or municipality | Improved health, dignity, and economic activity; sustainable water services |

### Assumptions and risks

A good ToC also states **assumptions** (what must hold for the logic to work) and **risks** (what could break the chain). Examples: *“Households will connect if we extend the network”*; *“Old networks may burst under higher pressure.”* Making these explicit helps with risk management and realistic target-setting.

[STEP] Logic Models — visualising the framework

## Logic Models

A **Logic Model** is a **visual** (often one-page) representation of the results chain. It usually shows:

- **Inputs** (left) → **Activities** → **Outputs** → **Outcomes** → **Impact** (right),
- with **Assumptions** and **External factors** noted underneath or to the side.

Logic Models are used to:

- **Communicate** your programme’s logic to council, Treasury, and oversight bodies.
- **Design indicators**: each level (especially outputs and outcomes) needs measurable indicators.
- **Check alignment**: does our IDP priority and SDBIP objective match this logic? Does our budget fund these inputs and activities?
- **Evaluate**: at the end of the year, we compare actual outputs and outcomes to the model and explain variances.

### Logic Model vs Theory of Change

- **Theory of Change** is often a narrative and can be more detailed on the “why” and “how.”
- **Logic Model** is typically a one-page diagram or table. Many municipalities use the terms together: the Logic Model is the simplified, visual form of the ToC.

[STEP] Hands-on: Sketch the levels for a programme you know

## Hands-on: Sketch the Results Chain for a Programme

**Tool:** Pen and paper, or **Google Sheets** / **Excel**.

1. Pick **one** programme or project you know (e.g. water expansion, revenue collection, LED, waste, by-law enforcement).
2. Create a simple table with 5 columns: **Inputs** | **Activities** | **Outputs** | **Outcomes** | **Impact**.
3. Fill in **2–4** bullets per column. Be concrete:
   - **Inputs**: Budget amount (or “as per budget”), staff (e.g. “2 project officers”), systems (e.g. “billing system”).
   - **Activities**: “Lay 15 km pipe,” “Install 400 meters,” “Run 4 community awareness sessions.”
   - **Outputs**: “500 new connections,” “20 km pipe,” “1 000 meters.”
   - **Outcomes**: “Reduced interruptions,” “Higher payment rate,” “Fewer complaints.”
   - **Impact**: “Improved access to water,” “Sustainable services,” “Better health.”
4. Add **one assumption** and **one risk**.
5. **Use this:** This is the raw material for a Logic Model. In the **Practical** section we will turn it into a clearer one-pager. In the **AI Application** section we will use AI to generate a first draft from a short description.
